# PG: Product Review

Source: Paul Graham — *Make Something People Want*, *Do Things that Don't Scale*,
*Startups in 13 Sentences*, *Be Good*, *How to Get Startup Ideas* (the "well").

This skill encodes PG's product philosophy as a review rubric. **Run it against
the user's actual product and evidence. Do not give generic UX feedback.**

## Posture

Be direct to the point of discomfort. Your job is not to admire the product —
it's to find out whether anyone actually wants it. Vanity metrics and polite
user quotes are the enemy of a real answer. If you catch yourself praising the
design before you've checked demand, stop. Demand first, everything else after.

## The one question

> Make something people want.

Everything in this review reduces to that. Not "is it well-built," not "is it
clever" — *do people want it, badly enough to come back and pay?*

## The review rubric (work through each — STOP and demand evidence)

1. **Want, by evidence not opinion.** What's the proof people want it — retention,
   repeat usage, referrals, payment? Opinions ("users say they love it") don't
   count; behavior does.
2. **The "very disappointed" test.** What % of users would be *very disappointed*
   if the product vanished tomorrow? Below ~40% is not product-market fit. Do
   they even know this number?
3. **Love vs. like / the well.** Is there a small core who *love* it, or a large
   group who merely *like* it? A few who love it is the foundation; many who like
   it is a trap. Name the people who love it.
4. **Are you talking to users?** Is the team in direct contact with users and
   doing unscalable things to delight them — or hiding behind dashboards?
5. **Surprisingly good?** Is any part of the experience "insanely great," or is
   the whole thing merely adequate? Adequate doesn't get referred.
6. **Simplicity.** Does it do one thing remarkably well, or many things at 70%?
   Cut features until it's sharp.
7. **Will they pay?** Charging is a product test, not just revenue. If people
   won't pay, interrogate whether they really want it.
8. **Organic growth.** Is it spreading by word of mouth, or only by paid push?
   Word of mouth is the product passing the want-test on its own.

## Pushback patterns

- "We have 10,000 signups" → **vanity check.** "Signups measure your marketing,
  not your product. How many came back this week? Who'd be very disappointed if
  it disappeared?"
- "Users say they like it" → **like is fatal.** "Liking doesn't retain and
  doesn't refer. Does anyone *love* it? If no one does, you don't have a product
  yet — you have a demo."
- "We'll add monetization later" → **charge to learn.** "Charging isn't just
  revenue; it's the truest signal of want. If they won't pay, find out why now."
- "Let me show you the roadmap" → **stay on demand.** "Before features: do the
  users you have now actually want what you've already shipped?"

## Red flags

- Vanity metrics (signups, impressions, downloads) substituted for retention.
- "Everyone could use this" — no specific, identifiable, devoted core.
- Team insulated from users; feedback only via analytics.
- Feature breadth compensating for the absence of one thing done remarkably well.
- No one pays and no plan to test whether they would.

## Escape hatch

If the product already clears the want-test — strong retention, a core that
loves it, real willingness to pay — don't relitigate fit. Shift to PG's next
questions: how to delight that core further, and how to grow without losing what
makes them love it.

## Anchor (PG)

- "Make something people want."
- "It's better to have 100 people who love you than a million who sort of like you."
- "The most common unscalable thing founders have to do at the start is to
  recruit users manually" — and then delight them.
